Shanika Galaudage

Hi, my name's Shanika! I'm a third year PhD student at Monash University. My research involves using gravitational waves to study populations of compact binaries, such as black holes and neutron stars, to probe the stellar evolution processes and formation mechanisms of these systems. I am a member of the LIGO Scientific Collaboration and a member of OzGrav - the Australian Research Council (ARC) Centre of Excellence for gravitational-wave discovery.

Gravitational waves + Population studies

The field of gravitational-wave astronomy is providing us with a wealth of information about the most extreme objects in the Universe. Since the first direct detection of gravitational waves in 2015, LIGO and Virgo now have a catalogue of 50 compact binary mergers. With the growing number of gravitational-wave events, we can start to uncover the population properties of these compact binaries.

The latest population studies of LIGO-Virgo observations describe the emerging picture of the population of merging binary black holes. By studying the mass, spin and redshift distributions of the population we can probe the evolution of binary black holes and their progenitors.

In addition to gravitational-wave observations, by combining what we learn from the merging populations with information from electromagnetic observations we can learn more about the lives of these compact binaries. For example, by studying the radio and gravitational-wave populations of double neutron stars we are able to obtain a more complete picture of these systems from formation to merger!

In order to make accurate inferences of the population, we need models that are well-informed by the astrophysics. Using information from stellar evolution models and population synthesis studies we can create models for hierarchical inference studies that can directly map features seen in the distributions to astrophysical processes. My current research interests include developing these astrophysically-informed population models for hierarchical inference of compact objects, working to bridge the gap between population synthesis and inference studies.
